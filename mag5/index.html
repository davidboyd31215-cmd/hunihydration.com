Section 1The 30-Second Read

The headline: The four hyperscalers (MSFT, GOOGL, META, AMZN) committed roughly $650–725B in 2026 capex, almost all of it for AI infrastructure. Apple is the outlier — it shipped cash back to shareholders instead of pouring concrete. Every cloud company on its call said the same thing: compute constrained.

  • Money flows in a tight loop: NVIDIA (GPUs), TSMC (custom silicon for TPU / Trainium / Maia / MTIA), HBM memory (SK Hynix, Micron, Samsung), networking (Broadcom, Arista, Marvell), nuclear and grid power (Vistra, Constellation, Oklo, NextEra, X-energy), and construction / land for new data centers.
  • Apple ran the opposite playbook: $100B fresh buyback authorization, $0.27 dividend (up 4%), $37.0B already repurchased in the first half of FY26. No multi-hundred-billion AI capex line.
  • Two earnings "beats" had asterisks: Amazon's $30.3B net income includes a $16.8B pre-tax gain on its Anthropic stake. Alphabet's results included a $37B equity-securities gain. Meta's net income was inflated by an $8.0B tax benefit. Strip those out and earnings are narrower than the headlines.
  • Stock reaction split (Apr 30 close): META –9% and MSFT –6% (capex jitters on the $190B ramp); GOOGL +10% (cleanest AI-revenue evidence); AMZN essentially flat (+1%, AWS reacceleration offset capex concerns); AAPL +3% in extended trading on the buyback news.

8.2   What the Money Buys

  • GPUs (NVIDIA). Single biggest line. Blackwell and Blackwell Ultra ramp.
  • Custom silicon. Google TPU v7 (Trillium / Ironwood), Amazon Trainium 3, Microsoft Maia 200, Meta MTIA — all fabricated by TSMC, mostly on N3 / N3P.
  • HBM3e memory. The actual supply bottleneck. SK Hynix, Micron, Samsung. Component pricing was Microsoft's stated reason for adding $25B to its 2026 capex.
  • Networking and ASIC partners. Broadcom, Arista, Marvell. Marvell co-designs Maia.
  • Data center shells, land, fiber, cooling. Heavy concentration in Northern Virginia, central Ohio, Phoenix, the PNW, and Iowa.
  • Power. The newest constraint. See below.

Section 9Who Is Receiving It

9.1   NVIDIA

NVIDIA Q4 FY26 (period ended late Jan 2026, reported Feb 25): revenue $68.1B (+73%), data center $62B (+75%). Hyperscalers were >50% of data-center revenue. FY26 total revenue $215.9B. Q1 FY27 guidance $78B, well above the $72.6B consensus.

9.2   TSMC — the common denominator

Key insight
Every custom chip on this list (TPU, Trainium, Maia, MTIA) is fabricated by TSMC, mostly on N3 or N3P. So is most of NVIDIA's lineup. TSMC sits at the bottom of the funnel and collects from all four hyperscalers no matter who wins the chip race.

Section 10Risks and Watch Items

  1. Free cash flow squeeze Amazon TTM FCF fell 95% to $1.2B as capex tripled. OCF is fine; FCF is collapsing because every dollar is redeployed. If revenue growth slows even slightly, the math breaks.
  2. Capex is increasingly debt-funded Alphabet issued $31.1B in senior unsecured notes this quarter. The buybacks stopped. The cash isn't there to do both. Watch leverage ratios next quarter.
  3. ROIC question is real The market punished META (–7%) and AMZN (–3%) on capex announcements even with revenue beats. Microsoft's $37B AI run-rate and Google Cloud's $460B backlog are the cleanest proof points. Meta and Amazon are still mostly promise.
  4. Component pricing, not unit volume Microsoft's $25B capex hike was explicitly the same chips and memory at higher prices. HBM and N3 wafer pricing is doing the talking. TSMC and SK Hynix are pricing accordingly.
  5. One-time gains are flattering reported earnings Strip Meta's $8B tax benefit, Alphabet's $37B equity gain, and Amazon's $16.8B Anthropic mark-up, and the EPS beats are far less impressive. Watch operating income, not net income.
